Part I · The idea

A tontine is survivorship, pooled

Invented by Lorenzo de Tonti in 1653: a group pools capital; everyone draws income while alive; when a member dies, their share stays with the pool — so the income of the living grows. The last survivors are carried by everyone who came before. No insurer guarantees anything; the pool is the guarantee.

1653 · Born

Tonti pitches Louis XIV: state borrowing where coupon payments concentrate on survivors. European states fund wars with it for 150 years.

1900 · Peak

Tontine-style ("deferred dividend") policies are two-thirds of US life insurance — about 7% of national wealth sits in them.

1905 · Killed

New York's Armstrong Investigation finds insurers embezzling the opaque pools. The product dies of custody, not arithmetic.

Part I · The idea

Mortality credits: the highest yield nobody can print

Each death releases an estate to the survivors — actuaries call these mortality credits. They rise with age exactly when portfolio returns stop being enough, which is why a tontine can pay a lifetime income with no insurer, no guarantee capital, and no margin. Part I · The idea

1905: the pot was dark, and hands went in

The Armstrong Investigation didn't find bad math. It found that decades-long pooled funds, visible to no member, were being skimmed by the companies holding them: embezzlement, self-dealing, and abusive lapse terms engineered so members forfeited.

  • Opaque custody — no member could see the pool.
  • Discretionary payouts — the insurer decided who got what, when.
  • Forfeiture by fine print — miss a premium, lose everything.

New York then regulated 95% of the US market, and the product vanished — “throwing out the baby with the bathwater,” as the Cato Institute's retrospective puts it. Note what the statute actually bans: payout intervals longer than a year. Monthly-paying tontines were never the target.

The three 1905 failures — and what fixes each is structural, not moral: a ledger anyone can read, rules with no discretion, and lapse that pauses instead of confiscating.

Part II · The hard part

A blockchain fixes custody — and knows nothing about death

Smart contracts solve exactly the three Armstrong failures: transparent custody, mechanical rules, no discretion. But a tontine's entire trigger is a fact the chain cannot observe: whether a human being is still breathing. Every onchain tontine lives or dies by how it imports that one bit of reality.

Part III · The design

Import death as evidence, not as authority

Long Bread's answer: the world already emits cryptographically signed statements about life and death — every email is DKIM-signed by its sending institution. Funeral homes, obituary platforms and insurers send machine-generated notifications; your own mail provider signs everything you send. zkEmail turns any such email into an on-chain proof that reveals a fact without revealing the mail. No oracle committee. No operator. Evidence.

Part III · The design

Proof of life: a code word that didn't exist yesterday

Each season the contract derives a fresh nonce from a blockhash observed after the season begins. To check in, a member sends one email with that nonce in the subject line and proves their provider's DKIM signature over it.

  • Can't be stockpiled: the nonce didn't exist before the season — an heir can't replay emails written while you were alive. (The demo hit this rule for real: a proof written seconds before a season roll was correctly rejected.)
  • Can't be delegated to a bot… cheaply: it requires live control of the mailbox each season, forever.
  • Missing one costs nothing: income pauses; a lapse never forfeits. Lesson 2, structurally enforced.
  • Lost wallet ≠ death: re-prove the same mailbox to bind a new wallet.
Part III · The design

Proof of death: bonded, optimistic, refutable by one check-in

1Report
Anyone holding a death-notification email from an allow-listed institution (funeral home, obituary platform, insurer) posts it as a zk proof + a good-faith bond. One claim per unique email.
→
2Waiting period
Nothing moves. The claim is public. The reported member can kill it with a single ordinary check-in — and the bond is forfeited to them.
→
3Finalize
Unchallenged claims become final; the reporter's bond returns and they're recorded for the finder's fee.
→
4Share out
Anyone triggers settlement: family share → named beneficiary, finder's fee → reporter, remainder → survivors by the fair-transfer weights.

Why a waiting period is non-negotiable

False death records are routine at scale: the US Social Security Death Master File wrongly marks ≈12,000 living people dead every year. A design without a refutation window replicates that harm with money attached.

Why this shape works elsewhere

It's the optimistic-oracle pattern (bond → liveness window → dispute): UMA resolves 99.8% of assertions without any dispute. Here the natural challenger is the “dead” member — the one party guaranteed to care.

Members with no email trail at death are handled by presumed death: a very long silence, a much bigger bond, a longer window.

Part IV · The economics

The fair transfer plan (Sabin–Fullmer)

When a member dies, who gets their estate — and how much? Splitting equally cheats the old; splitting by stake cheats the poor-but-old. The verified answer from the actuarial literature: split in proportion to

wi  =  si · qi/(1−qi) · (1−βi)
stake · nominal tontine yield (q = your annual death probability) · pooled fraction (β = your family share)

With these weights, every member's expected gain from others' deaths equals their expected contribution from their own — the “fair bet” condition. It's what lets a 30-year-old and an 85-year-old sit in one circle without either subsidising the other, and it's implemented as an O(1) accumulator (the per-event group-gain factor), so settlement never loops over members.

Equal stakes, no family share. Drag the ages — the estate split follows q/(1−q), not opinion.

Part V · The honest part

Strengths and weaknesses, side by side

Where it's strong

  • The 1905 failure is structurally gone — custody is a public contract; no discretion anywhere; no promoter take.
  • Life ≠ activity — the first onchain tontine whose liveness measures biology-adjacent evidence, not key possession.
  • Forgetting is safe — lapse pauses income; only verified death moves money; wallets are recoverable via the mailbox.
  • Fairness is verified math — Sabin–Fullmer weights (checked 3–0 against primary sources), bequest handled per Bernhardt–Donnelly, mixed ages/stakes provably non-subsidising.
  • Dispute economics with teeth — every attack path funds its victim or its hunter.
  • It exists — live on Gnosis; a full member lifecycle (join → check-in → report → dispute → settle → income) already executed on mainnet.

Where it's weak

  • Demo proofs are simulated — the Groth16 verifier is a mock today; contract rules are real, cryptographic email verification is not yet.
  • Mailbox custody = identity custody — an heir with full, ongoing email control can impersonate check-ins; the nonce and bounty make it costly and risky, not impossible.
  • A trusted set remains — the DKIM registry owner/guardian (an EOA in the demo) decides which institutions count.
  • The email substrate is uneven — not every death emits an institutional email; the presumed-death path is deliberately slow.
  • Small circles are lumpy — mortality credits arrive as events, not a smooth curve; the math wants hundreds of members.
  • Regulatory posture is untested — structured to avoid insurance (no underwriting, no guarantee, monthly payouts) per the TontineTrust analyses, but no court has said so.

The lifecycle that already ran on mainnet

Two members, Maria (b. 1958) and Bianca (b. 1949), joined Harvest Circle II from burner wallets. Bianca was falsely reported dead — and cancelled it with one check-in, pocketing the reporter's bond. Reported again and unchallenged, her passing became final: 2% to the reporter, 30% to her named beneficiary (Maria), the remaining 68.6% to the circle. Maria's balance grew from 0.60 to 1.01 xDAI, she checked in, and drew her first month's income. Every step is a public transaction — and each one is a recording in the guide.

Part VI · On chain, today

The path from demo to institution

1 · Real circuits

Per-blueprint Groth16 verifiers (registry.zk.email compiles and deploys from regex blueprints) replace the mock via one setVerifier call each. The proof envelope, registry and pools don't change.

2 · Harder identity

Mailbox-grade Sybil resistance graduates to eID-grade anchors (government / bank mail domains, or zkPassport-style personhood) for production circles.

3 · Shared stewardship

DKIM registry + oracle ownership to a timelocked multisig with an independent revocation guardian; then circles of hundreds, where the law of large numbers does the actuarial smoothing.
